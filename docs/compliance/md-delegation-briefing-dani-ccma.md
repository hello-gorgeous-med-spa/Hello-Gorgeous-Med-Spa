## 2. Legal frame — what Illinois allows an MD to delegate

Under the **Illinois Medical Practice Act (225 ILCS 60/54.2)** (summarized for discussion):

1. A physician may delegate patient-care tasks to an **unlicensed person** (CCMA qualifies as unlicensed personnel) who has **appropriate training and experience**.
2. A **licensed health care professional** practicing within their Act must be **on site to provide assistance**.
3. Delegated tasks must be within **your** (the MD’s) education, training, experience, and within a real **physician–patient relationship**.
4. You **may not** delegate a task that is **statutorily reserved** to a physician.
5. Delegation may be via oral/written/electronic **orders, protocols, standing orders, guidelines**.

IDFPR med-spa guidance adds, in substance:

- Cosmetic injectables, many energy devices affecting living tissue, medical lasers/RF, microneedling, non-superficial peels, etc. are **practice of medicine**, not esthetics.
- Medical procedures must be performed by a licensed physician **or** by proper **physician delegation** to appropriately trained persons with required supervision.
- Estheticians **cannot** perform those medical procedures under an esthetics license; if somehow assisting under MD delegation, they **must not hold out** as doing esthetics.

**Nurse Practice Act note:** Medication administration rules for unlicensed staff are **stricter** under nursing law. Prefer that injectables/med administration stay with **MD / RN / NP** unless counsel + malpractice explicitly approve a written CCMA injection protocol (unusual for aesthetic toxins/fillers).

### 3A. Illinois MA injection nuance (for Dr. Clint)

Secondary practice guidance (e.g. med-assistant summaries of IL scope) often states that **MAs may give intramuscular, subcutaneous, and intradermal injections** when:

- delegated by a **licensed physician** (not by RN as the primary delegator — Nurse Practice Act limits RN → unlicensed med admin), and  
- under **direct supervision with the physician / authorized practitioner present in the facility**.

That guidance also typically excludes:

- **IV** start / IV medication  
- **anesthetics**  
- often **controlled substances**

**IDFPR still lists vitamin injections as medical med-spa services.** So this is not “Dani can shoot vitamins whenever.” It is: **Dr. Clint may choose to put specific IM/SQ vitamin protocols on her CCMA delegation when he is on site and all conditions above are met.** Confirm with Illinois healthcare counsel + malpractice — do not rely on blog summaries alone.
